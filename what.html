Going to the Internship Fair

I'd never thought I'd have the courage to go to the internship fair, but, before I knew it, my second year at Bradley slammed into me like a runaway car. Most of my friends already had internships and work in their fields of study. Meanwhile, I could barely do chores without messing up, let alone hold down an actual job. I realized that if I wanted to do something with my life, I'd have to change.

I remember walking into the foyer of the Renaissance Colosseum that fateful day. Everywhere I looked were people in business suits, carrying shiny briefcases, their faces glowing with the confidence of corporate executives. I felt sick to my stomach, unsure if the feeling was courage, nervousness, or dread. It was like staring into the face of a titan that was about to grab me up in and eat me. I was surrounded by people who were better than me in every way, and yet, they were my competition, my opponents, my enemies.

When I entered the arena, a thousand thoughts poured into my head about my profile, my projects, the employers and what the companies did. I wanted to just close my eyes, tune them out, and go into my happy place. When I walked up to the first employer, I could barely say my class year and degree. My mind went blank, my introduction collapsed before it even got off the ground. The man bombarded me with questions: "What languages do you know? Do you have any work experience? Describe to me a project you did in Java." My thoughts flowed through my mind like a waterfall, and I just couldn't keep up with them.

When it was over, I wanted to throw up. I just wanted to run out that door and never come back again. A voice screamed in the back of my head: "You're a sophomore and you can't even design a webpage! If you can't get a little internship, Microsoft and Google would never hire you. You're a failure as a computer scient-"

The voice abruptly stopped, just as a hand landed on my shoulder.
